An on-screen typeface for the visually impaired.
Designed by Laura Pitt

ABOUT

In a world that is continually progressing digitally and at an exponential rate, the use and understanding of technology is expected. For awhile, screens have been getting smaller and smaller, in which designers must figure out a way to  display information so that it is easy  for the user to comprehend and read -- a huge part of this is affected by the typefaces that are used on an interface.

LORETTA SERIF addresses these issuses that those with visual impairments struggle with using a larger x-height, slightly wider proportions, as well as lengthening ascenders and descenders. The typefaces that inspired this typeface were LORA and RETINA.

Simple Gestalt created by spaces in the past did indeed find preference for serif typefaces.

Tinker commented that perceived legibility was due to great extent to familiarity with typeface. 40 years ago, sans serif typefaces were not as common as they are now, and if studies were repeated, it would not be surprising to find completely different results.

ink/light traps
following Retina, these indents allow greater legibility at smaller font sizes
driving serif
following Lora, these serifs are placed on the right side of each leg. There is also a curve of the leg that goes inwards to emphasize its direction.
slanted serif
The slight slant in the serif subtly reinforces movement throughout body text.
